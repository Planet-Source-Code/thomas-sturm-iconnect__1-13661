Introduction

so, what is it good for, then? well, Iconnect is a so-called IP-publisher. It publishes your current IP by uploading it on an ftp-server. Hmm, you don´t seem too impressed. How about "it enables you to access your computer anywhere, anytime, even though you´re only using a slow dial-up connection". Sounds better?

hardware requirements

All you need is a computer that supports "wake up on modem call" via BIOS - or rather "Advanced Power Management" (APM). This should be the case for any vaguely modern computer: 1998 and up will do. If you´re not sure, check it out: while your computer is booting, it shows the following message:

Press <del> to enter SETUP

(or something similar). Go ahead, press "del" and see what happens. You should be confronted with a jungle of options...don´t panic! although, at this point, I somehow feel compelled to utter the usual sermon: "this software is published without any warranty whatsoever..." I don´t. We´re adults, aren´t we? the actual names of these options might vary from one BIOS to the other, but the one we´re keen on should be found among something like

Power Management Setup

(Award-BIOS). Once you got there, you should find an option called

PWR up on Modem Act

after marvelling at the capitalization there, use the "page up" and "page down" keys to change it to "enabled" - if that´s not already the case. Follow the instructions of your BIOS manufacturer to save the changes and reboot. If you didn´t change anything else, you´ll be back in windows a minute later. Turn your modem off and shutdown your system. When your computer is off, simply switch your modem ON again. Your system should now boot up, too. If that´s not the case but your computer supports APM, it would be time to consult your mainboards manual: might be a jumper to be set but I can´t help you there.

software requirements

You´re still reading? well, this means nothing less than you being able to boot your computer from anywhere by just calling your own phone number. Now, at http://www.tridiavnc.com (or http://www.uk.research.att.com/vnc/, for that matter) you will find a free (open source!) exiting tool, which lets you remotely access your computer via any JAVA-capable browser. You simply get to work with your desktop through someone elses browser. Without having to install anything on the remote computer (and even if the remote computer is running Linux/Unix or whatever). Get it and install it, is what I would suggest.

...and what do I need your program for?

Now the only thing that´s missing is a link: you can remotely boot your computer, but...it doesn´t go online by itself, does it? and if so, you still don´t know it´s IP-adress. And even if you did, what happened if you were on the other end of the world but for some reason couldn´t connect your computer (and therefore couldn´t disconnect it from the internet, let alone shut it down again). And then, like every screen-oriented remote admin tool, VNC works most efficient with low resolution and 8 or 16 Bit colour depth - but that´s not a good enough reason to always only use 800 * 600 pixels, 16 Bit? well, now we´re talking. I hope you will like this.

Unzip and run. If your modem is OFF, absolutely nothing will happen. This is a feature :-)
If your modem is ON, however, you might see the following:

Options

All options in Iconnect can be easily set by double-clicking on the file Iconnect.ini in your Iconnect-folder. Clicking on the left button

is supposed to have the same effect: it will open Notepad with the file Iconnet.ini:

[UserPrefs]
language=deutsch
ExitClose=Opera,winvnc,Outlook
AutoShutdown=0
Optimize=0
UseNetlaunch=0
Netlaunch=C:\Programme\NetLaunch
Server=D:\Internet\Vnc\WinVNC.exe
LocalPort=5800
ScreenRes=

[Connection]
ISP=
FTPHostname=ftp.xoom.com
FTPUsername=homihot
FTPPassword=open
FTPTimeout=0:2:0
RemoteFile=index2.htm
MaxRetries=5

[Timer]
Interval=300
Timer1=0:0:5
Timer2=0:5:0
Timer3=0:10:0

[UserPrefs]

This section, who would have thought it, describes how you want to customize Iconnect to your needs:


language=deutsch : this should be obvious. Set it to language=english to "anglisize" the little app. In order to "translate" Iconnect into whatever language you like, have a look at the language.ini file: it has two sections right now. Add "your" language as a section and translate the few words you find there. Now you can add "your" language into Iconnect.ini. If no language is specified here, Iconnect will speak english. The "help"-button in Iconnect will currently only work with the "build-in" languages. In order to make it work with "your" language as well, you´d have to translate this document into "your language" and save it to the /Docs-directory. If you completely customized Iconnect to a lnguage other than english or german, please let me know!

ExitClose=program1,program2,... : some programs really frown upon the request to close. In case you want to automatically run one of those everytime you connect to the internet, it would be a pity if it displayed a dialog daying "Are you really, really sure you want to close this wonderful program?" ("No", "Cancel") - and no one would be there to marvel at it. Since this would stop your computer from shutting down, you can tell Iconnect to close these programs before shutting down your computer. Note that the name of the program(s) as they appear in their main window don´t have to be fully known; Iconnect will close all windows where these names can be found. The entries of the list are separated by a ",".

AutoShutdown=0 or 1 :if "0", Iconnect will quit leaving your computer ON. If "1" (or, in fact anything larger than "0") your computer will shutdown when the following conditions apply:

  1. dial up failed: it took longer than the time specified in [Timer]Timer2 (default 5 minutes) to connect to your ISP.
  2. ftp upload failed: it took longer than the time specified in [Connection]FTPTimeout (default 2 minutes) to connect your ftp-server.
  3. ftp busy: if there is only the data left to be send and the server won´t respond, Iconnect will retry (MaxRetries=) times before shutdown.
  4. you couldn´t connect to your computer: if the "reset countdown" button is not being pressed after [Timer]Timer2 (default 5 minutes).
  5. you lost an existing connection: rest countdown was pressed, but [Timer]Timer3 (default 10 minutes) expired.
  6. and finally, closing the program (or clicking on "close session") after it went online shuts down your system, too.

bottom line: if you´re using Iconnect to dial up your computer and connect to it, use AutoShutdown=1. If your playing around with it´s settings (or it´s source code :-) you might want to turn this option off.

Optimize=0 or 1 :if "0", Iconnect will leave your windows wallpaper/desktop pattern/fontsmoothing settings alone when connecting. If "1" (or anything larger than "0"), it will turn all this off while connecting. This is useful if your remotely using your computer via WinVNC or something similar: the less data has to be transferred, the better the response of your desktop. A single color background might not have as much impact on performance than a lower screen resolution but still makes a huge difference.

UseNetlaunch=0 or 1 :Netlaunch is a wonderful little program. It sits in your systray and makes every dial-up connection of your computer available at your fingertips. You can launch or close programs before, when or after you connected. You can schedule connections or interrupt them if they´re too slow. All this for a couple of KB of memory. It´s freely available at Blackcastle Software and, well, Iconnect works neatly with it :-) highly recommended due to several err...irregularities in wininet.dll, which Iconnect would normally use.

Netlaunch=(Program Path) "C:\Programme\NetLaunch" is an example path pointing to your Netlaunch folder. Since Netlaunch can be "installed" like Iconnect, i.e. by simply extracting it into a program folder, it leaves no "trace" in your registry. This in turn means, if you want Iconnet to use it, you´ll have to enter the path here. Note: if UseNetlaunch=1 and there is no path specified, Iconnect must be in the same folder as Netlaunch. Makes sense, btw.

Server=(Program Path) "D:\Internet\Vnc\WinVNC.exe" is an example for an application to load after Iconnect has done it´s duty. This can be a *.bat file, vbscript, windows PWS (personal webserver) or whatever you can think of (as long as the file specified here actually exists!).

Localport=0...65535 : port you want to connect to. Iconnect will create a default webpage where your computer is linked like this: <a href="http:127.0.0.1">127.0.0.1</a>. Specifying a "Localport=123" has the following effect: <a href="http:127.0.0.1:123">127.0.0.1</a>. Makes it easier to "click and connect". 5800, as in the example, is btw. the default port WinVNC listens to.

ScreenRes=(width,height,bits per pixel) : if you try to connect with, say 1600*1200 Pixel in 32-Bit true-color, even the best connection will be too slow to actually work smoothly with your computer. Worse, some remote-admin tools (like IBM Desktop-On-Call 2.0, for instance) might crash straight away if you bother them with more than 800*600. For a modem connection, a setting like ScreenRes=800,600,16 gives good results - especially in connection with Optimize=1. If all you want to do is to start your webserver, you can ommit this option and thus use your normal screen setting. Note: Iconnect actually will reduce your color-depth (bits per pixel) without rebooting. As a result, this will screw up your desktop colors. Might look alarming (or quite artistic if you go from, say, 1024,768,32 to 640,480,8) but is completely harmless. In any case, your default screen settings will be restored before the program quits.

[Connection]

ISP=(Service Provider) : useful only in connection with Netlaunch. You can specifiy your internet provider here as it appears in the Netlaunch menu (or rather, like in your dial up connection window). In this case, you do not have to specifiy any default provider as described in the quickstart-section.

FTPHostname=(Name) : name of the ftp-server you want to upload your IP-address to. Note: there´s no leading ftp:// before the name.

FTPUsername=(Name) : login name for that server.

FTPPassword=(Name) : password for your account, please.

FTPTimeout=(Time) : default 0:2:0 (2 minutes). After connecting to the internet, Iconnect tries to contact FTPHostname. It actually needs to establish 2 connections to this server: one for sending and receiving data, and one for bossing it around. This should usually work within a few seconds. If it takes more than 2 minutes for instance (FTPTimeout default), it´s definately better to stop trying.

RemoteFile=(Name) : file you want to upload to the server. If a file with the same name does not exist in your Iconnect folder, Iconnect will "upload" date, time and current ip in an HTML-"file" that gets created only on the server. It´ll look like this:

There is, however, a file called "template.htm" in your Iconnect folder. It consists of the key-words "local date:", "local time:", "current ip" and a simple HTML-table. If the Name after "RemoteFile=" matches a file in this folder, it will be uploaded to the server. Iconnect will insert the correct date, time and IP after the appropriate "key-words" and in addition fill up the table top to bottom with the data of your recent connections. Thus, you can use Iconnect to display the most recent connections (or phonecalls) to your computer. Since it will only do that if the table is empty, the actual HTML-file can be as sophisticated as you want. The table can have as many (empty) entries as you need, though once it is full you will have to empty it manually since Iconnect will not overwrite any cells that are already filled. In short: you can customize the HTML-page you actually upload to your hearts content; if any of the key-words are missing, the corresponding data will not be published. Feel free to experiment.

MaxRetries=(Number) : number of times Iconnect will try to persuade the ftp-server to cooperate after the actual connection to it has been established. In other words, the whole connection business can be summarized as: try to get any response from the server until FTPTimeout. If you didn´t, quit. If you did, try to login and establish a data connection. If that fails, retry the whole thing "MaxRetries" times. Default is 5. If that didn´t work, quit.

[Timer]

Interval=(Number) : Iconnect uses the standard timer event in Windows. You can specifiy here, how often this will occur. Interval is in ms. The shorter the interval (the lower the number), the more accurate the timing of Iconnect gets. Of course, that means more system resources as well. The default (300) actually works so well that I am beginning to wonder why I left the option to change it in there. Well...

Timer1=(Time) : Countdown before dial up. Default 5 seconds. If you put a link to Iconnect into your "Autostart"-folder of your start menu, you propably don´t want to really go online every time you boot up your computer while your modem is on. If your modem is off, nothing will happen anyway. But if you´re like me, you propably power on your computer using any odd switch: be it the modem, the space-bar...just don´t forget to hit "enter" if you see Iconnect appearing on your screen. Especially if AutoShutdown is turned ON. I suggest setting it to a minute or so.

Timer2=(Time) : Countdown after Iconnect has uploaded your HTML-"page". Default 5 minutes. If you know how long it takes for your computer to boot (say, 1 minute 30 seconds) and for the upload to be finished (max. 30 seconds) this gives you a "time-window" of 2 to 7 minutes to connect to your computer until you see your desktop and press "enter". Iconnect will hop diligently into your taskbar and sit there until 10 seconds before the countdown is over. If you get phonecalls very often, but actually connect your computer very rarely, reducing this time might be a good idea. Your phone bill will thank you.

Timer3=(Time) : Countdown after the first reset of the "stopwatch". Default 10 minutes. Every 10 minutes, Iconnect will pop up again, 10 seconds before the countdown expires, and ask for you to hit "enter" again. In case for whatever reason that doesn´t happen, you´ll be propably grateful to know your computer won´t stay online.
